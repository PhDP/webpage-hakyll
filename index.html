--- title: About ---

I'm a computational scientist working on machine learning, a branch of Artificial Intelligence. My work is focused on inductive transfer, which in layman's terms could be described as the capacity to transfer knowledge from one task to another. It comes naturally to us, e.g.: we use what we learned for walking when learning to run, but almost all machine learning algorithms build models from scratch. Not only is it inefficient, but being unable to rely on a vast and solid web of prior knowledge makes machine learning models vulnerable to overfitting and unable to deal with new situations where little data is available. Furthermore, when conditions change, it is hard to know if the model is still valid. I mostly use the rich (and complex!) data from ecological systems to develop and test new approaches to inductive transfer.

I'm a Ph.D. candidate at the Theoretical Ecosystem Ecology laboratory (Université du Québec) and the Quebec Center for Biodiversity Science (McGill U.). My work is supported by an Alexander Graham Bell Graduate Scholarship from the NSERC. I'm a member of the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers and their Computational Intelligence society.

I love programming! I was mostly programming in C and C++ but 2013 marks my transition to static functional languages (F#, Haskell).
